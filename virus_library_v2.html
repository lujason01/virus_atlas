Classification & Serotypes

Adenoviruses are classified into seven species (A–G) comprising 57 human serotypes. They are further divided by hemagglutination properties and receptor usage (CAR, CD46, or desmoglein-2).

Species A (12,18,31) Species B (3,7,11,14,16,21,34,35,50) Species C (1,2,5,6) Species D (8,9,10…) Species E (4) Species F (40,41) Species G (52)
Structural Features

The icosahedral capsid is formed by 240 trimeric hexon proteins and 12 pentameric penton bases at each vertex, from which fibre proteins project outward. The fiber knob mediates receptor attachment. A core of dsDNA (~36 kb) is condensed by histone-like proteins VII and V.

Discovery & History

1953
Wallace Rowe, Robert Huebner and colleagues at the NIH isolated adenoviruses from adenoid tissue cultures undergoing spontaneous cytopathic effect. Initially called "adenoid degeneration (AD) agent."
1954–1956
Multiple independent groups, including Hilleman and Werner, isolated similar agents from military recruits with acute respiratory disease. The family Adenoviridae was formally established, unifying these isolates.
1962
Trentin, Yabe and Taylor demonstrated that human adenovirus 12 (HAdV-12) induced tumors in newborn hamsters — the first demonstration of oncogenic potential in a human virus, opening an entire field of tumor virology.
1979–present
Adenovirus vectors become a cornerstone of gene therapy and vaccine delivery. The COVID-19 pandemic saw their large-scale deployment in the Oxford-AstraZeneca and Johnson & Johnson vaccines (ChAdOx1, Ad26).

Serotypes & Variants

Thirteen naturally occurring serotypes (AAV1–AAV13) with distinct tissue tropisms determined primarily by the capsid surface. AAV2 was the first to be extensively characterized; AAV9 crosses the blood-brain barrier efficiently.

AAV1 — muscle, CNS AAV2 — broad AAV5 — CNS, lung AAV8 — liver AAV9 — CNS, heart AAV-DJ — engineered AAVrh10 — CNS
Structural Features

The ~26 nm T=1 icosahedral capsid is assembled from 60 subunits of three overlapping capsid proteins: VP1, VP2, VP3 in a ~1:1:10 ratio. Unique regions of VP1/VP2 on the interior carry a phospholipase A2 domain essential for endosomal escape. The 5' hairpin ITRs fold into T-shaped secondary structures that initiate replication.

ssDNA · ~4.7 kb

Discovery & History

1965
Bob Atchison, Bill Casto, and Wallace Hammon discovered small virus-like particles contaminating adenovirus preparations at the University of Pittsburgh. Named "adeno-associated virus" for its dependence on adenovirus co-infection.
1984
Srivastava and colleagues sequenced the AAV2 genome, revealing the ITRs and the bipartite rep/cap organization that would become the foundation for recombinant vectors.
1990s–2000s
Development of recombinant AAV (rAAV) vectors by deleting rep and cap and replacing them with therapeutic transgenes. The ITRs alone are sufficient for packaging and long-term episomal persistence.
2017–2023
Landmark FDA approvals: Luxturna (AAV2, RPE65 mutation), Zolgensma (AAV9, SMA), and Hemgenix (AAVrh74, hemophilia B). AAV becomes the dominant in vivo gene therapy platform.

Classification & Risk Groups

HPVs are classified by L1 sequence identity. Over 200 types infect humans, stratified by oncogenic risk. High-risk types (α-HPVs) encode E6/E7 oncoproteins that degrade p53 and pRb.

HR: 16, 18, 31, 33, 45 HR: 52, 58 (common in Asia) LR: 6, 11 (condylomata) Cutaneous: 1, 2, 3, 4 EV types: 5, 8
Structural Features

The T=7d icosahedral capsid consists of 360 copies of L1 (major capsid protein) arranged as 72 pentamers, and ~12–72 copies of L2 (minor capsid protein). L1 pentamers are stabilized by intermolecular disulfide bonds. The circular dsDNA genome (~8 kb) is associated with cellular histones.

Genome Map

Discovery & History

1907–1933
Ciuffo (1907) first demonstrated the transmissibility of human warts by cell-free filtrate. Shope (1933) showed that cottontail rabbit papillomavirus (CRPV) caused warts — the first virus proven to cause benign tumors.
1970s–1983
Harald zur Hausen at DKFZ identified HPV DNA in cervical cancer tissue. His 1983–84 papers cloned HPV16 and HPV18 and demonstrated their consistent presence in cervical carcinomas, establishing the causal link.
2008
Harald zur Hausen awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery that HPV causes cervical cancer.
2006–2014
FDA approvals of Gardasil (quadrivalent, 2006), Cervarix (bivalent, 2009), and Gardasil 9 (nonavalent, 2014). Vaccines based on L1 virus-like particles (VLPs) — no live virus required.

Classification & Serotypes

VSV belongs to genus Vesiculovirus within Rhabdoviridae. Two main serotypes: VSV-Indiana (most studied, prototype strain) and VSV-New Jersey. Indiana type includes subtypes Mudd-Summers (VSIV) and Orsay.

VSV-Indiana (VSIV) VSV-New Jersey (VSNJV) VSV-Alagoas Cocal virus Isfahan virus
Structural Features

VSV has a distinctive bullet shape formed by a tight helical nucleocapsid (RNA+N protein) coiled within a matrix (M protein) layer, all enclosed in a host-derived lipid envelope studded with ~1200 trimeric G protein spikes — the sole surface glycoprotein mediating fusion and entry via LDL receptor family members.

Genome Map

Discovery & History

1925
First described as the causative agent of vesicular stomatitis in cattle and horses in the United States. The disease causes vesicular lesions on the tongue and feet, resembling foot-and-mouth disease — prompting careful epidemiological distinction.
1962–1970s
VSV became a premier model system for studying RNA virus biology due to its rapid growth, simple 5-gene genome, and tractability. Studies by Huang, Baltimore, and colleagues defined the negative-sense RNA replication cycle and the role of the L protein RdRp.
2005
Recombinant VSV (rVSV) vectors developed as vaccine platforms. The rVSV-ZEBOV (Ervebo) Ebola vaccine uses VSV-Indiana with its G protein replaced by Ebola virus GP — approved by FDA/EMA in 2019.

Classification

SFV is a prototypic member of the Alphavirus genus, closely related to chikungunya (CHIKV), Ross River (RRV), and Sindbis (SINV) viruses. Alphaviruses are arboviral — transmitted by arthropod vectors (mosquitoes). SFV itself is non-pathogenic to humans.

Semliki Forest (SFV) Sindbis (SINV) Chikungunya (CHIKV) Ross River (RRV) Eastern EEV Venezuelan EEV
Structural Features

An icosahedral T=4 nucleocapsid (240 copies of capsid protein) is surrounded by a host lipid bilayer through which project 80 trimeric spike complexes of heterodimeric E1/E2 glycoproteins. E2 mediates receptor binding; E1 contains a class II fusion peptide that drives low-pH endosomal fusion.

Genome Map

ssRNA(+) · ~11.4 kb · mRNA-sense

Discovery & History

1942
Isolated by Smithburn and Haddow from Aedes abnormalis mosquitoes collected in the Semliki Forest of western Uganda. Named for its site of discovery. The virus showed neurotropic properties in experimental animals.
1960s–1980s
SFV became a model for studying alphavirus biology. Ari Helenius and Kai Simons used SFV to elucidate lipid bilayer structure and membrane protein assembly, earning it prominence in membrane biology research.
1991–present
Kenneth Lundstrom and colleagues developed SFV as a high-level expression vector. The subgenomic promoter allows massive structural protein overexpression. rSFV vectors deployed for vaccine antigens, protein production, and oncolytic applications.

Classification & Strains

Vaccinia belongs to the genus Orthopoxvirus, which also includes variola (smallpox), monkeypox, and cowpox. Multiple VACV strains exist with varying virulence: Western Reserve (WR) for lab use, Modified Vaccinia Ankara (MVA) as an attenuated vaccine vector, NYVAC, and Dryvax (historic smallpox vaccine).

WR (Western Reserve) MVA (Modified Ankara) NYVAC (attenuated) Lister/Elstree IHD-J strain
Structural Features

VACV produces multiple infectious forms: IMV (intracellular mature virion) — the most abundant, with a biconcave core; IEV (intracellular enveloped virion, double-wrapped); CEV/EEV (cell-associated/extracellular enveloped virions). The IMV membrane contains ~20 viral proteins. The genome (~190 kb linear dsDNA) is entirely replicated and transcribed in the cytoplasm, carrying its own transcription machinery.

Discovery & History

1796
Edward Jenner performed the first successful vaccination, inoculating James Phipps with cowpox material to protect against smallpox. The term "vaccination" derives from vacca (Latin: cow). The exact origin of vaccinia virus remains debated — possibly horsepox or a hybrid.
1939
Barnard documented the morphology of vaccinia by electron microscopy — among the first viruses visualized. VACV's large size (~360 nm) made it visible even by light microscopy, earning the name "elementary bodies."
1980
The WHO declared smallpox eradicated — the only human disease ever eradicated. VACV was the vaccine that made this possible; over 200 years of mass vaccination eliminated variola virus from natural circulation.
1982–present
Bernard Moss and colleagues developed recombinant VACV as a vaccine vector for foreign antigens. MVA (Modified Vaccinia Ankara) — a highly attenuated strain — became a central platform for HIV, malaria, and COVID-19 vaccine candidates. Imvamune/Jynneos (MVA-based) approved for mpox prevention.
